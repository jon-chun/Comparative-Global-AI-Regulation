### 2.1 Chatham House Endnote Style

Endnotes use numbered superscript markers. Format examples:

- **Journal article:** Lawrence Freedman, 'The Atlantic crisis', *International Affairs* 58: 2, Summer 1982, pp. 13--17.
- **Book:** Anu Bradford, *The Brussels Effect: how the European Union rules the world* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2020), p. 42.
- **Chapter:** Laurence D. Weiler, 'No first use: a history', in David N. Schwarz, ed., *NATO's nuclear dilemmas* (Washington DC: Brookings, 1983), pp. 100--12.
- **Subsequent:** Bradford, *The Brussels Effect*, p. 55.
- Avoid *ibid.* and *loc. cit.*; use short titles.

## 4. Strategic Plan

### 4.1 Core Thesis: The Cyber Governance Trilemma

**Central argument:** AI regulation is the defining cyber governance challenge of the 2020s. The three major jurisdictions -- EU, China, US -- each resolve the tension between innovation, safety, and regulatory interoperability differently, creating a **trilemma** in which no jurisdiction can simultaneously optimise all three:

| Jurisdiction | Prioritises | Sacrifices |
|---|---|---|
| EU | Safety/rights + interoperability | Innovation speed |
| US | Innovation speed + (partial) interoperability | Safety/rights consistency |
| China | Innovation speed + safety (selective) | Interoperability |

This regulatory fragmentation is itself a **cyber risk**: inconsistent AI governance creates gaps in critical infrastructure protection, complicates international cyber norm processes, and forces global firms into compliance arbitrage.

### 4.2 Why AI Regulation Is a Cyber Policy Problem

Arguments to make in the Introduction:

1. **AI-powered cyber threats** -- deepfakes, autonomous social engineering, AI-generated malware, voice fraud
2. **AI in critical infrastructure** -- AI systems now control energy grids, financial systems, telecommunications; their regulation determines cyber resilience
3. **Data governance convergence** -- AI regulation (EU AI Act) intersects with data governance (GDPR) and cybersecurity law (NIS2 Directive); they cannot be analysed in isolation
4. **Autonomous weapons and cyber conflict** -- AI governance frameworks will shape the rules for autonomous cyber operations
5. **Regulatory fragmentation as systemic risk** -- divergent AI rules create seams that sophisticated threat actors can exploit
